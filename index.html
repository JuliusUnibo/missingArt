Missing art

The National Socialist regime defamed many artworks as "degenerate".
For this reason, between 1933 and 1945 numerous paintings were lost forever.

But not just art created by persecuted groups was affected. The Nazis also robbed
cultural treasures from jewish owners, Polish people, Sinti and Roma, and other minorities.

This web project shows the enormous damage that hatred, racism and antisemitism had
on the cultural heritage of us all. What kind of art are we missing today?

When

Compared to Italy, German fascism was only in power for twelve years. Another striking difference between the two political systems was their cultural policy.

Modern art was an object of hatred for the National Socialists. Not only were countless artists persecuted, but also owners of the paintings were targeted.

This graph shows how deep the wounds were that National Socialism tore into our cultural heritage. Many missing canvases are several hunderd years old.

The oldest missing painting dates back half a millenium

The data used for this web project was obtained from the German Centre for Lost Cultural Property / Deutsches Zentrum f√ºr Kulturgutverluste.

Nearly 2000 canvas paintings are still missing today

Violet dots represent artworks with known year of manufacture. Blue dots stand for unsecure years, in which missing canvas paintings were made.

This project analysed a total of 1842 artworks, which the authorities registered as lost due to Nazi persecution. The data was linked to Wikidata and europeana.

Where

The regime in Germany did not end at its borders. In the twelve years between 1933 and 1945 the Nazi's cultural policy caused losses of artworks from all over the world.

Berlin occupies the starting point from every shown graph. The lines connect the known work locations from the affected painters with the headquarter of the regime.

Most missing canvasas were European

Albeit the huge range of the cultural destruction of facism, the data also shows clearly that mostly art from European painters was effected by the persecutions.

From whom

This graph shows the 25 artists who who lost the most paintings because of the persecutions between 1933 and 1945.

The regime's cultural policy had large-scale consequences

Evidentally, the data quickly registers only one lost painting per artist. From the total of 1842 lost canvases, not many were created by the same person.

The Nazis did target individual artists. Prominent examples in this graph are Max Liebermann or Gustav Klimt. Even Vincent van Gogh shows up.

A still life painter as target of Nazi persecutions?

However, the consequences of the racist and Antisemitic politics had a large-scale effect. Among the artists with the most lost paintings are many lesser-known names.

Noteworthy is also Johann Georg de Hamilton, who clearly leads the statistic. Why so many artworks of the early modern still life painter are missing, is unclear.

What is clear, is that also many art owners were persecuted by the regime and their paintings or art collections were stolen.

Which art style

The graph only shows the twelve most counted art styles which could succesfully be connected to the persecuted artists via Wikidata.

Many missing paintings are from the Baroque period

It is no surprise to find Expressionism and also Impressionism at the top of this chart. Both art styles represent the modernity, frenetically hated by the regime.

However, among the artists affected by the persecutions were also many painters from the Baroque era or even some Rococo-artists.

Assigning a painter to a cultural movement should always be a case-by-case decision and open to discussion. Nevertheless, the data shows an interesting trend.

What to do

This web project visualized the huge effect the German National Socialist rule had on the global culutral landscape.

The National Socialist regime did not destroy everything

Racist and Antisemitic hate not only destroyed numerous artworks. Many paintings are still missing today.

As expected, most of the artists affected by the losses came from Europe and can be categorised as belonging to the artstyles targeted by the regime.

However the data clearly showes how extensive the destruction was. As even the data indicates, also the art owners were tracked down.

What can I do now?

While many of these works of art will never be found again, you are free to look at other paintings from the persecuted artists.

Here you can find the six online repositories which collect the most artworks from the affected painters according to the database eurpeana.
